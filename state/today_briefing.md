# Daily AI Recruitment Briefing — 21 July 2026

## AI in Recruitment — What's New

- A **ResumeTemplates.com survey of hiring managers** found 48% would now rather invest in AI tools than hire and train a recent college graduate, 55% have shifted at least part of their entry-level hiring budget to AI, and 23% plan to hire fewer 2026 grads or none at all — a signal to redesign early-career pipelines around AI-augmented roles before next cycle, not bolt AI onto existing headcount plans. ([Class of 2026 College Graduate Hiring Report](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/class-of-2026-college-graduate-hiring-report-302828328.html)) (17 July)
- Cutting the other way, **JLL's Future of Work survey** of 2,200+ C-suite and corporate real-estate leaders found most expect AI to grow their workforces (60%) and reinvent rather than replace human roles (60%) — with the most AI-advanced firms leaning into full-time hiring and entry-level investment, even as only 15% say they've reached an "optimizing" stage of adoption. Read alongside the grad-hiring data, it frames the real planning question: not whether to hire, but which roles to redesign first. ([JLL](https://www.jll.com/en-us/newsroom/ai-redesigns-jobs-not-cuts-them)) (14 July)

## Worth Reading

- A mid-year compliance analysis maps the tightening state patchwork governing AI in hiring — transparency notices, bias-audit and human-oversight expectations spreading across jurisdictions as federal disparate-impact enforcement recedes. A useful checkpoint for pressure-testing where documented human review actually sits in your stack before the next state law takes effect. ([2026 Midyear Hiring Compliance: Building For A Changing Legal Landscape](https://www.forbes.com/sites/alonzomartinez/2026/07/20/2026-midyear-hiring-compliance-building-for-a-changing-legal-landscape/)) (20 July)

**Bottom line:** The workforce data is pulling in two directions — employers cutting grad reqs while leaders forecast growth — so use this week to decide which entry-level roles you redesign around AI versus retire, and keep bias audits and human-review documentation current as the state-law patchwork hardens.
